  • Angle between the sun and the horizontal plane.
  • Represents the sun's height in the sky.
  • Measured in degrees above the horizon.
  • 90 degrees: sun directly overhead, 0 degrees: on the horizon.
  • Essential for evaluating solar potential at a specific location and time.

Pyranometer for Measuring Solar Radiation:

Measures total solar radiation reaching the Earth's surface, including direct and diffuse sunlight.

Working Principle: Uses a thermopile sensor covered with a glass dome. Solar radiation heats the sensor, generating a voltage output proportional to radiation intensity.

Units of Measurement: Readings in watts per square meter (W/m²), typically per hour (W/m²/hr).

Calibration: Requires periodic calibration to maintain accuracy.

Applications:

  • Weather and climate monitoring.
  • Solar energy system design and monitoring.
  • Agriculture and environmental research.

Types of Pyranometers:

  • First Class (high-precision).
  • Second Class (general meteorological use).
  • Secondary Standard (specific research applications).

Sun shine recorder

A sunshine recorder is a device used to measure the amount of sunshine at a given location. Sunshine is defined as direct beam radiation from the sun reaching the earth's surface.

Glass ball - Focuses sunlight onto card

Card holder - Holds cards that record burn traces

Tracking - Clockwork mechanism follows sun's path

Housing - Protects from weather; allows sunlight

When beam sunshine strikes the spherical glass lens, it focuses and burns a trace onto the graduated cards mounted vertically behind it. The length of the burn traces is measured and totaled to calculate sunshine duration for that period. The card holder simultaneously moves at a constant speed on a 24 hour clock to account for changing position of the sun.

The sunshine recorder provides reliable data on sunshine hours and variability over time, allowing analysis of historical trends at a given geographical location.

Solar Distillation

Process that utilizes solar energy to produce fresh and potable water from saline or impure water sources, such as seawater, brackish water, or contaminated water. It's a simple, sustainable, and effective method for water purification and desalination, especially in areas with limited access to clean drinking water.

Process:

  1. Solar Collector: A sloped, transparent surface traps sunlight.
  1. Water Supply: Saline or impure water is placed below the collector.
  1. Evaporation: Sunlight heats the water, causing it to evaporate, leaving impurities.
  1. Condensation: Water vapor rises and condenses on the cooler collector surface.
  1. Collection: Purified water collects in a separate container.

Advantages:

  • Purification: Effectively removes salts and contaminants.
  • Sustainability: Uses solar energy, making it eco-friendly.
  • Reliability: Works in areas with limited access to electricity.
  • Low Operating Costs: Minimal maintenance and operating costs.
  • Versatility: Applicable at various scales.

Limitations:

  • Low Production Rate: Slower compared to other methods.
  • Weather Dependency: Efficiency depends on weather conditions.
  • Space Requirements: Requires ample space for equipment.

Carnot Cycle

Theoretical thermodynamic cycle representing the most efficient process possible for converting heat energy into work, consisting of reversible isothermal and adiabatic processes.

Carnot Cycle Features:

  1. Reversibility: Idealized reversible processes.
  1. Efficiency: Sets the maximum efficiency for a heat engine.
  1. Isothermal Processes: Heat transfer at constant temperature.
  1. Adiabatic Processes: Includes adiabatic expansion and compression.

Carnot Cycle Limitations:

  1. Idealization: Assumes reversible processes, not realistic.
  1. Practical Implementation: Challenges in achieving near-Carnot efficiency.
  1. Finite Heat Capacities: Assumes infinite heat reservoir capacities, impractical.
  1. Engine Design: Difficulties in realizing ideal processes in actual engines.
  1. Material Constraints: Assumes ideal gas, not always practical.
  1. Limited Applicability: Theoretical insights may not apply universally.

Working of Hot Dry Rock (HDR) Geothermal Power Plant:

The Hot Dry Rock (HDR) geothermal source, also known as Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS), involves creating a geothermal reservoir in hot impermeable rock by stimulating fractures. The process includes the following steps:

  1. Drilling: Two wells drilled into hot impermeable rock - injection and production wells.
  1. Water Injection: Cold water injected into the rock to create fractures.
  1. Heat Extraction: Fractures filled with pressurized hot water as it circulates.
  1. Production Well Operation: Pumped hot water brought to the surface through the production well.
  1. Power Generation: Hot water used to produce steam that drives a turbine for electricity.
  1. Reinjection: Cooled water reinjected into the rock for the cycle to repeat.
